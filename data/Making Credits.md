Making Credits

Making credits is essentially the point of the whole game. With them, you can buy ships, upgrades, weapons, and armor, as well as other items. There are a ton of ways to earn credits. listed below.

## Missions

![](./File:Credits.png)

Factions provide quests that provide credits on completion. These quests vary from assaulting an enemy base to resupplying outposts to retrieving intelligence reports from spies. Missions are one of the most easily-understood ways of making credits, and are an attractive option for newer players because you don't need any credits to start one. Missions also have the added bonuses of increasing your Standing and Loyalty Points with the faction who gives it.

See Missions for further information.

## Mining

![](./File:KorreliteRef.png)

Mining asteroids is another one of the simplest and most consistent ways of making credits in Starscape, since it is heavily focused on in the tutorial.

While mining can be done in any ship with weapons, ships with dedicated mining weapons are much faster - it's recommended for new players to start by using an Badger. While NPCs will buy any materials mined for a minimal price, materials are needed to build all later game ships or upgrade, so they can always be sold for more to other players (usually at the Citadel market).

Mining in Core and Secure systems is safe, for the most part, but only low quality, common ores will spawn. Mining is much more profitable when done in Contested and Wild systems, though this is more dangerous due to the presence of Pirates and Drones, and the ever-present threat of a hostile player appearing. The best method for mining in wild space is to fly to a distant area and use Scanning Probes to find asteroid Anomalies, as rarer ores can be found there and there are no NPC enemies to worry about.

For advanced mining techniques that involve multiple Roblox accounts, see Multiboxing.

## Hauling

![](./File:Blue_spice.png)

While hauling is not very effective early on, since newer player do not have the massive freighters available late-game, it can be very profitable when done correctly. Players can haul spice, supplies, and mission items.

Mission items are hauled to destinations specified by the mission agent. Spice is purchased from spice platforms or Frontier Outposts and then sold across the galaxy for maximum profit.

Military Faction supplies are picked up from faction Staging Areas and delivered to Contested System HQ stations for credits and Loyalty Points/Standing. Mercantile Faction supplies are purchased from the mercantile faction's capital and then sold to any Contested HQ station for high profit.

Purchasing spices from a spice station or FO will cause the price to go up, and selling spices to a station will cause the price to go down - this means that repeatedly using the same spice route (or using common spice routes) will slowly make it less profitable.

### WH/FO Spice Hauling

A notable form of spice hauling is Wormhole/Frontier Outpost hauling, or WH/FO hauling for short, which involves finding a wormhole that leads to wild space, and then finding a Frontier Outpost nearby.

Using the wormhole allows you to skip the vast majority of the time spent hauling, and make exceptionally profitable gemstone spice trades in just a few warps. Using a large ship, these routes can be usually be "exhausted" (by raising the FO prices high enough that the trip isn't worth it) in around half an hour to an hour, making well over 100k credits in the process. This makes it by far the most effective means of earning credits, though only if you can find a suitable wormhole.

## Trading

Trading with players is a viable method of making credits, as there are many players that require materials but are unable to find them. It should be used in conjunction with mining in order to get cheaper ores, alongside allowing one to sell ores at a higher price than what the Citadel offers, especially for Axnit, Narcor, and Red Narcor, which often sell for a decent price per piece.

## Piracy

There are two ways of reliably getting money, namely selling/refining scrap and whatever is in their cargo. Generally the former is very reliable when compared to the latter, although the latter can easily net someone enough resources for even entire warships.

You can pirate by using a combat ship and destroying ships that warp into an unsecure or wild system. To stop people from simply warping out, just launch a warp disruptor, or use the disruptor ability. It works best on miners or haulers as they have little ability to fight back.

You may also want to ransom a player. While generally a bad choice if they are armed and have something like beams, due to the loss of cargo on player death, getting a good ransom can easily give you great profit. Demanding credits can be even better, especially against haulers who don't usually carry valuable items but usually have a large supply of credits from spice trading.

## Drone Hunting / Drone Fleets and Drone Factories

Drone hunting is easy to pick up even in a Stratos, but the further out from the Core you go, the stronger and faster the drones become, and more types of drones can spawn. For a new player starting out, a mid tier Fighter or Interceptor are good enough ships to hunt drones as they have decent mobility and damage output, although players who are serious about hunting Drones will soon look for an upgrade. Drone drops vary from Junk and Materials and scrap to exotic-level Ship Upgrades. This makes the profit from Drone hunting semi-luck based, but higher level enemies in Unsecure / Contested and Wild systems will more commonly drop higher quality goods in a larger amount compared to in Secure space. While missions to destroy Drones can be taken, the amount of credits these missions give will usually pale in comparison to the resources gained from even a short Drone hunting trip.

![](./File:Aaaaaaaaasfdfsff.webp)

An example of a Drone Fleet's commanding drone loot.

As players progress through the game, they will be able to afford more robust combat ships such as Corvettes and Frigates which will make the process of hunting Drones far easier, as these larger warships possess higher damage reduction values, larger amounts of health and increased sustained DPS compared to regular starcraft. Once players obtain their first warship, it becomes much more feasible to take on **Drone Events** such as Drone Fleets and Drone Factory. Drone fleet events are easily identifiable by the 2-5 circles with ship icons that appear on the top of your screen when warping into a planet's warp hub or ring. These circles serve as both the indicator for the amount of "waves" Drone fleets have as well as the current progress through the Drone fleet event. Drone fleets can spawn in all systems other than Core (Green) systems, although the further out from Core space players venture, the more common it is to find larger and more powerful Drone fleets. In order to advance through a Drone fleet, players must defeat sequential "waves" of drones, marked by a text box near the top of the screen which tracks the amount of Drones left to destroy before the next wave spawns in. Once players reach the final wave, a Drone Command Ship (marked by a white and yellow color palette) will spawn in which drops the majority of the Drone fleet's loot. This loot can range from low-tier materials and ores to high-tier refined ores and even exotic ship upgrades and blueprints, with better loot coming from stronger fleets.

For more information on Drone Fleets and their respective tiers, please visit Drone Fleets.

Drone factories, on the other hand, do not have a screen indicator like Drone fleets while in their vicinity, and players will need to manually look around to see if there is a Drone factory nearby their planet's warp hub / ring. Drone factories differ from Drone fleets in that while they don't spawn additional waves when all the drones surrounding them are destroyed, they will spawn Drones whenever a section of them is destroyed.

Drone factories consist of three main groups of destructible sections; Shield Generators, Power Regulators, and Main Reactors. Each group of destructible sections must be destroyed in order to be able to damage the next group of sections, and attempting to damage a section before all of the previous sections are destroyed will result in that part of the Drone factory taking zero damage; this is also reflected by hovering over that particular section, with a small "Invulnerable" tag appearing. (Example; in order to begin damaging power regulators, players must first destroy all the shield generators a drone factory has).

![](./File:Static-assets-upload5672749500759745195.webp)

An example of a Tier 1 Drone Factory in secure space.

For each individual part of a Drone factory that is destroyed, an additional wave of Drones will spawn in to defend the Drone factory. This can be **Extremely Dangerous** for newer players -- as compared to Drone fleets which can only have one fleet per wave, focusing on destroying the Drone factory's modules as soon as possible will soon cause you to be surrounded by dozens of angry Drones which can shred even a Destroyer-class ship's health in a matter of seconds. When a Drone factory's Main Reactor is destroyed, the Drone factory will undergo a short explosion animation before spawning in a Drone factory wreck, which contains a large amount of resources with a small chance of dropping exotic Ship Upgrades.

For more information on Drone Factories and their tiers, please visit Drone Factory.

## Scavenging

The least consistent, and arguably worst way of collecting credits, Scavenging is simply traveling from system to system, exploring wreckages, and BF anomalies for any items of use. You can find ship mods, guns, armor, and other items that can be sold to players through the marketplace. If done with a warship you can drone hunt as well. Similar to mining, loot obtained by scavenging gets better in systems farther away from the core with a more dangerous security status.

Keep in mind that you will mostly find low level loot, e.g. tier 1 ship mods, rifles, etc, which typically sells for a few hundred credits. As a result, this is only recommended for new players.

## Bounty Hunting

If a player is killed by another player, they may place a Bounty on the attacker. Anyone who kills the attacker will gain a portion of the Bounty equal to the worth of the ship they destroyed. Gaining credits this way is unreliable due to how large the map is and the possibility of the attacker having a stronger ship.

## X-structure hunting

There is a very small chance that an X-aberration is a satelitte structure, which contains an ancient beacon. These contains a clue that leads to the location of an intact X-structure, which contains high level loot and lots of resources.

*For more information on X-structure hunting, check out the X-structure hunting page.*

## Standing Rewards selling

Standing Rewards can be purchased from faction terminals. You can find the terminals at stations in the faction's territories or in a HQ. It's a fast way to earn credits if you have lots of standing points from missions or protecting contested system and turning in special items. For example: you complete a mission for core sec for 100 standing points , you can buy 4 holograms that cost 25 points and can sell for 500 credits , 4 times 500 = 2k credits with the credits you get from the mission in the first place. But you can sell for even more if you use your points to buy blueprints that can be sold to the player market for a higher price.
